# 7. Variational and Hybrid Quantum Algorithms

## 7.1 Background
Variational and hybrid quantum algorithms emerged as a major response to the limitations of noisy intermediate-scale quantum hardware. Rather than relying on deep fault-tolerant circuits, these methods combine parameterized quantum circuits with classical optimization loops.

The variational quantum eigensolver and the quantum approximate optimization algorithm became two of the most visible paradigms in this area. Their appeal lies in their relative hardware accessibility and their adaptability to chemistry, optimization, and other applied settings.

## 7.3 Why this family became important
This family of algorithms became central in the NISQ era because it offered a concrete path for using imperfect devices. It also helped reorient the field from purely asymptotic algorithmics toward resource-aware and hardware-constrained algorithm design.

## 7.4 Challenges
Despite early enthusiasm, variational methods face major open challenges, including barren plateaus, optimizer instability, noise sensitivity, scalability issues, and uncertainty about the extent of achievable quantum advantage over strong classical methods.

## 7.5 Section summary
Variational and hybrid quantum algorithms are among the most practically motivated directions in current quantum algorithm research. However, their long-term significance will depend on whether they can deliver robust, scalable, and demonstrable advantages under realistic benchmarking conditions.
