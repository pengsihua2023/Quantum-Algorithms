# Introduction

Quantum computing has attracted sustained attention because it offers a fundamentally different model of information processing from classical computation. By exploiting superposition, interference, entanglement, and coherent unitary evolution, quantum computers may solve certain problems more efficiently than classical machines under well-defined assumptions. Within this broader field, quantum algorithms occupy a particularly important position, because they determine whether the distinctive features of quantum mechanics can be translated into concrete computational benefit.

The study of quantum algorithms developed through several major stages. Early results such as Deutsch-Jozsa and Simon’s algorithm were significant because they demonstrated that quantum computation could exhibit nonclassical algorithmic behavior even in relatively abstract oracle settings. Later breakthroughs, most notably Shor’s factoring algorithm and Grover’s search algorithm, brought the field to much wider prominence by showing that quantum computation could yield dramatic advantages for problems of broad mathematical and practical interest. These developments established quantum algorithms as a serious research area within theoretical computer science, mathematics, and physics. [FOUNDATIONAL REF]

Over time, the scope of the field expanded considerably. What began as a collection of striking isolated examples developed into a rich body of work spanning simulation, phase estimation, amplitude amplification, linear algebra, optimization, variational methods, and machine-learning-related proposals. At the same time, the field also became more internally diverse. Some directions remained rooted in rigorous complexity-theoretic results and long-term fault-tolerant quantum computing, whereas others were shaped by the practical limitations of noisy intermediate-scale quantum devices and therefore emphasized hybrid and heuristic algorithmic strategies. [RECENT REVIEW]

This expansion has made a fresh review of quantum algorithms increasingly valuable. First, the literature has become too broad to be captured adequately through a narrow focus on a few famous examples. Second, recent years have highlighted the importance of distinguishing between different meanings of “progress.” A new algorithm may be significant because it proves a stronger asymptotic separation, because it introduces a reusable computational primitive, because it is more compatible with near-term hardware, or because it clarifies the limitations of an existing approach. Third, as excitement surrounding quantum computing has grown, it has become especially important to separate theoretical promise from realistic computational feasibility. Not all claimed quantum speedups are equally meaningful in practice, and their interpretation often depends on hidden assumptions concerning input access, oracle construction, state preparation, measurement cost, and hardware scalability. [CLASSICAL COMPARISON REF]

The purpose of this review is therefore not merely to list representative quantum algorithms, but to provide a structured synthesis of how the field has developed, what its major conceptual families are, where its strongest achievements lie, and what major challenges remain unresolved. In particular, this review emphasizes four themes. First, quantum algorithms should be understood through a clear classification of problem types and algorithmic mechanisms rather than through a sequence of isolated historical anecdotes. Second, rigorous theoretical speedups remain central to the intellectual significance of the field, but they must be interpreted alongside realistic resource requirements. Third, the distinction between NISQ-era algorithms and fault-tolerant algorithms is essential for evaluating present-day claims. Fourth, fair comparison with strong classical baselines is indispensable for judging practical relevance. [CITATION NEEDED]

The remainder of this review is organized as follows. We begin by introducing the foundational aspects of quantum computation most relevant to algorithm design. We then summarize major historical milestones and examine the principal classes of quantum algorithms, including search, algebraic, simulation-based, linear-algebraic, variational, and optimization-oriented methods. Next, we discuss the practical constraints and resource considerations that shape the field, followed by a broader comparison with classical algorithms. Finally, we outline open problems and future directions, with particular attention to the relationship between formal quantum advantage and practical computational utility. [CITATION NEEDED]
